Why I Ski

I grew up in Utah, which means I basically grew up on the mountain. Utah is home to 15 world-class ski resorts packed into one of the most accessible corridors of mountains in the world — the Wasatch Range, just a short drive from Salt Lake City.

What makes Utah skiing different is the snow itself. Light, dry, ultra-low-density powder — often called "champagne powder" — falls in epic volumes across the Cottonwood Canyons and beyond. Resorts like Alta and Snowbird average over 500 inches of snowfall per year.

Skiing teaches you something valuable: how to make quick decisions under pressure, how to read your environment, and how to push past your comfort zone while managing real risk. Those lessons have shaped how I think about business, leadership, and life.

This page is my love letter to Utah skiing — the runs, the resorts, the gear, and the data behind one of America's greatest outdoor sports cultures.

Utah's Best Resorts

Utah packs more world-class skiing into a small area than anywhere else in North America. Here are the standouts — each with its own personality, but all with legendary snow.

Alta

Little Cottonwood Canyon · 2,614 acres · ~550" avg snowfall

One of the oldest ski areas in the US. Skiers only. Known for technical terrain, deep pow, and a no-frills atmosphere that purists love.

Snowbird

Little Cottonwood Canyon · 2,500 acres · ~500" avg snowfall

Right next door to Alta. Bigger, faster lifts, and one of the longest seasons in the country — often open into May or June.

Park City

Summit County · 7,300 acres · ~360" avg snowfall

Largest ski resort in the United States (after merging with Canyons). Epic Pass resort with incredible variety for all ability levels.

Deer Valley

Park City · 2,026 acres · ~300" avg snowfall

The most luxurious ski experience in Utah. Immaculate grooming, impeccable service, and no snowboarders allowed. A different kind of special.

Powder Mountain

Eden, UT · 8,464 skiable acres · ~500" avg snowfall

North America's largest ski resort by acreage. Intentionally uncrowded and indie. If you want untracked powder runs, this is your mountain.

Brighton

Big Cottonwood Canyon · 1,050 acres · ~500" avg snowfall

Accessible, affordable, great for beginners and freestyle skiers. Night skiing available. One of the best bargains on the Wasatch Front.

The Gear Guide

Whether you're a first-timer or a seasoned local, having the right equipment makes every run better. Here's a breakdown of what you need — and what to look for.

Complete Ski Kit

  1. Skis
    • All-mountain skis (ideal for Utah conditions)
    • Powder skis — wider waist (100mm+) for deep snow days
    • Carving skis for groomed runs and groomers
    • Length: roughly your chin to top of head
  2. Bindings & Boots
    • Bindings matched to your skis and DIN setting
    • Stiff boots for aggressive skiers (flex 100–130)
    • Medium flex (80–100) for intermediate / all-day comfort
  3. Outerwear
    • Waterproof jacket with taped seams (20,000mm+ rating)
    • Insulated or shell pants — bibs offer better coverage
    • Base layer: merino wool or synthetic moisture-wicking
    • Mid layer: fleece or down for cold days
  4. Accessories
    • Helmet — non-negotiable, even for experts
    • Goggles: interchangeable lens for different light conditions
    • Gloves or mitts: waterproof with proper insulation
    • Neck gaiter or balaclava for cold storm days
  5. Safety (Backcountry)
    • Avalanche beacon (transceiver)
    • Probe and shovel
    • Airbag pack for high-risk terrain
    • AvaCheck app or Avalanche Center forecast

Insider Tips

  • Get to the mountain early — Alta and Snowbird open at 9am and powder gets tracked out fast.
  • The "Sweet Spot" window for Utah powder is usually 7–10am on a storm or day after.
  • Deer Valley will carry your skis to the lift. Just let them — it's part of the experience.
  • Brighton is 45 minutes from BYU in Provo. Perfect for a weekday morning class-skip.
  • Get a Ikon Pass if you ski Park City / Deer Valley; Epic Pass if you ski everywhere else.
  • Don't dismiss a small storm — even 6 inches on firm base is magic in Utah's dry powder.
  • Wax your skis regularly. Cold, dry Utah snow is actually harder on bases than wet East Coast snow.
